# Writing style guide

A reference for anyone writing content for the Mountain Heritage Trust
website — trustees, staff, freelance contributors, and anyone reviewing
copy before it ships. The guide describes how the Trust *already* writes,
distilled from existing blog posts, and codifies a few small consistency
fixes.

When a guideline conflicts with telling the story well, the story wins.
This is a guide, not a straitjacket.

---

## Voice and tone

The Trust's voice is **knowledgeable, warm, respectful, not breathless**.
Readers are assumed to be interested in mountains and climbing without
necessarily being expert.

- Confident and historically aware: place people, places and routes in
  context. Mention dates, eras, achievements without bragging.
- Warm but not gushing. We say "huge thank you" but follow it with a
  specific factual reason ("…who donated £425…"), not vague enthusiasm.
- Respectful of climbers and subjects, especially in obituaries and
  retrospectives. Open with a short, plain sentence ("We have just
  received the sad news that Doug Scott has passed away aged 79.") rather
  than rhetorical flourish.
- Not corporate. Avoid empty section headings like "Striving for
  greatness" or "A symbol of human achievement". Specific is better than
  inspirational.
- Not gallery-copy. We are a heritage charity, not a tourist board.
  Avoid "experience the wonder" / "embark on a journey" register.

## Person

- The Trust is referred to in the **third person** when stating facts —
  "Mountain Heritage Trust was founded in 2000…"
- **First person plural ("we")** when speaking on behalf of the Trust to
  the reader — "We have just received the sad news…", "We hear a great
  evening was had by all". Use this sparingly; default to the third person
  for substantive content.
- Subjects of articles (climbers, expeditions, donors) are always in the
  third person.

## Capitalisation

Always capitalised:

- **Mountain Heritage Trust** (full name) and **the Trust** (when
  shortened, capital T).
- **Trustees**, **Patrons** and **Ambassadors** when referring to those of
  the Trust ("Doug was one of our most long-standing Trustees").
  Lower-case generally ("a charity's trustees").
- **Chair**, **Patron**, **Ambassador**, **President**, **Vice-President**
  when used as a title attached to a person ("Chair of the Trustees",
  "President of the Alpine Club").
- Proper nouns: peaks, ranges, routes, expeditions ("South Face of
  Annapurna", "Salathe Wall", "Anti Atlas").
- **Sherpa** (proper noun, capitalised as ethnic group; also as title:
  "Pertemba Sherpa").

Lower case:

- Generic terms: a trustee, a patron, a chair (when not a specific title).
- Climbing styles when used descriptively: trad, sport, alpine-style,
  siege tactics, lightweight ascent.

Abbreviations:

- Introduce on first use with the full name, with an inline link if it has
  one: "[British Mountaineering Council](https://www.thebmc.co.uk/)
  (BMC)". Subsequent mentions can use the abbreviation alone.
- BMC, NLHF, RGS, CAN, MHT — established abbreviations the audience knows.

## Spelling

**British English** throughout. The Trust is a UK charity for British
mountaineering heritage; American or mixed spelling is a small but real
incongruity.

| British (use this)              | Avoid                          |
| ------------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| organise, organisation          | organize, organization         |
| recognise, recognition          | recognize                      |
| centre, theatre, fibre          | center, theater, fiber         |
| catalogue, dialogue             | catalog, dialog                |
| favour, behaviour, neighbour    | favor, behavior, neighbor      |
| metre (length), meter (device)  | mixing the two                 |

`-ise` rather than `-ize` endings.

## Dates and numbers

- **Dates**: `29 May 1953` (day, month-name, year). Avoid
  `May 29, 1953` (American). Avoid `29/5/53` (ambiguous).
- **Year ranges**: en-dash, no spaces — `1979–1982`, `1970s–80s`.
- **Decades**: `the 1970s` or `the '70s` (with apostrophe before the
  number). Avoid `the 70's`, `the 1970's`.
- **Heights**: digits + `m` with no space — `8,100m`, `7,250m`. Add a
  comma at the thousand for readability.
- **Currency**: £ symbol, no space, with thousands separator — `£100,000`.
- **Numbers in body**: spell out one to nine, digits from 10 onward
  (`five climbers`, `12 expeditions`). Always digits for ages, dates,
  measurements, money, and counts in the same sentence as another digit.

## Quotations

- **Curly quotes**, not straight: `"…"` and `'…'`. Most editors do this
  automatically; double-check on paste.
- Reported speech in **double** quotes; quoted material *within* a
  quotation in single. Period and comma go inside the closing quote.
- Attribute inline:
  > Terry Tasker, Chair of the Trustees, said, "Mountaineering and
  > climbing heritage is exceptionally varied…"
- For long-form quotes from books or letters, use a blockquote.

## Names and post-nominals

- First mention: full name, with post-nominals if relevant on first
  mention. Subsequent mentions: surname only, or first name where the
  voice is more personal (obituaries, ambassador profiles).
  > Sir Chris Bonington CVO, CBE, DL, founded the Trust… Bonington's
  > 1975 expedition…
- Honours come after the name, separated by commas, no full stops in the
  letters: `CBE`, not `C.B.E.`. The serial comma after the last honour
  is **omitted** when followed by a verb: "Doug Scott CBE was…".
- Living people: present tense for things they currently do. Past tense
  only for things they no longer do.
- Deceased people: past tense, full name + dates on first mention —
  "Joe Brown (1930–2020) was…".

## Climbing conventions

The audience is climbing-literate. We don't gloss climbing terms by
default ("first ascent", "trad", "siege style", "basecamp", "sirdar"
all stand). Glossing is reserved for:

- Niche or historical terms readers under 40 won't know.
- Where the meaning matters to the story for non-climbers.

Grades:

- UK trad grades: `E8`, `VS`, `HVS`, `XS` — uppercase, no space between
  letter and number.
- French sport grades: `7c+`, `8a`, `9a` — lowercase letter, plus is
  attached.
- Bouldering: `7C+`, `V12` — Font in upper case; V grades upper case.
- Be consistent within a piece.

Routes: italicise route names that are titles (`*The Big Bang*`,
`*One Day as a Tiger*`). Don't italicise mountain names or face names
("South West Face", "North Wall of the Eiger" — proper nouns, not titles).

Heights of peaks: include where useful and not redundant. A name like
"Annapurna" needs no height; "Trango Tower" benefits from one on first
mention.

## Images and captions

Every image needs:

- A descriptive **alt** attribute (filled in by the CMS field). Describe
  what's in the image, not "image of…". Example: "Doug Scott seated in a
  tent at Kangchenjunga base camp, 1979, surrounded by Sherpa team
  members." If the image is purely decorative, alt can be empty.
- A **caption** below the image. Captions are concise, sentence-case, and
  attribute the photographer:

  > Doug Scott (in tent) with Sherpas, 1979 Kangchenjunga expedition.
  > Photograph by Ian Smith.

- A **copyright line** at the very end of the post if any image needs
  one and the per-image attribution doesn't already cover it:

  > Cover image: copyright Ian Smith.

- Avoid "Image credit: Unknown" in captions. If the photographer is
  unknown, omit the credit rather than draw attention to the gap.

## Length and structure

- **Short announcements** (lecture notices, donations, thanks): one to
  three short paragraphs. No subheadings.
- **News** (acquisitions, exhibition openings, obituaries): three to ten
  paragraphs. Optional subheadings only if the piece exceeds ~600 words.
- **Long-form** (history pieces, retrospectives, in-memoriam): use `##`
  subheadings to break sections. Subheadings should be specific facts or
  named events, not abstractions ("The 1975 Everest expedition", not
  "An ambitious undertaking").

Open with context, not throat-clearing. The first sentence should give
the reader the news or the hook. Don't start with "It is with great
pleasure that…" or "We are delighted to announce that…" — get to it.

End with something concrete: a call to action, a fact, a quote, or a
detail. Avoid vague inspirational closes.

## Links

- Use inline links on the first relevant mention. Don't list URLs at the
  end.
- Link the **noun phrase** that names the thing, not "click here":
  `[The Climbers' Club](…)`, not `[click here for The Climbers' Club](…)`.
- External links open in a new tab is *not* the rule — the CMS handles
  this for content; we don't force it from inside markdown.

## House list (frequent names)

For consistency. When the official spelling differs, follow the
organisation's own spelling.

- Mountain Heritage Trust (the Trust)
- British Mountaineering Council (BMC)
- The Climbers' Club (with apostrophe)
- The Alpine Club, the Alpine Climbing Group
- Royal Geographical Society (RGS)
- The National Lottery Heritage Fund (NLHF)
- Community Action Nepal (CAN)
- Sir Chris Bonington CVO, CBE, DL
- Boardman Tasker Award for Mountain Literature (no hyphen between Boardman and Tasker)
- Kendal Mountain Festival
- Banff Mountain Festival
- Blencathra Field Centre (the Trust's home)

Place names with multiple acceptable spellings — pick one and stick with
it across the site:

- the Lake District (capitalised). Avoid "the lakes".
- Snowdonia / Eryri (use whichever the source uses; bias to the original
  English form where the rest of the piece is English-language).
- the Himalaya (singular, no s; "Himalayas" is acceptable but the Trust
  has been using the singular form).
- the Greater Ranges (capitalised collective term).

## Things we have caught and want to stop doing

Patterns observed in older content that should not be repeated:

- All-caps names at the start of bios: `JOE BROWN, was…`. Use sentence
  case: `Joe Brown was…`.
- Repeating the subject's name in the first sentence when it's already
  in the page heading.
- Stock corporate subheadings ("A Symbol of Human Achievement").
- Empty enthusiasm without facts ("an inspiring event for all").
- Voice slipping from third person to first ("He has done X. I am
  delighted to help…") within the same paragraph.
- Curly quotes mixed with straight quotes in the same piece.
- "click here" as link text.

## Where to apply this guide

- New blog posts and news.
- Edits to existing pages via Sveltia CMS.
- Team and patron biographies.
- Exhibition descriptions.
- About-page content.

The guide does **not** apply to:

- Quoted material — quotes are reproduced as the original speaker said
  or wrote them, even if they break a rule above.
- Press releases issued by other organisations and reposted.
- Legal text (privacy notice, terms of use).
